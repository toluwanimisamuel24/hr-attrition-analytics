# Executive Summary

**Project:** HR Workforce & Attrition Analytics
**Dataset:** 1,470 employee records across 3 departments
**Tool:** Microsoft Excel (Power Query, PivotTables, Dashboard)

---

## The Headline

This organisation is losing **16.2% of its workforce annually** — above the 10–15% industry benchmark for stable organisations. Across 1,470 employees, 238 left in the observed period. At a conservative replacement cost of 50% of annual salary, this represents approximately **$9.3M in annual, preventable expenditure**.

The attrition is not random. It is concentrated, patterned, and — critically — addressable.

---

## The Three Root Causes

**1. Unsustainable workload**
Employees working overtime leave at **30.6%** — nearly three times the rate of those who don't (10.4%). This is not a marginal difference. It is a structural signal that overtime, as currently distributed, is a retention liability.

**2. Early-tenure disengagement**
Attrition peaks in the **0–1 year tenure band** — meaning employees are leaving before the organisation recovers its hiring and onboarding investment. This is a preventable failure of early-career experience, not a long-term culture problem.

**3. Compressed entry-level compensation**
Attrition falls steadily as monthly income rises. The lowest income bands carry the highest attrition rates, pointing to a compensation structure that does not retain the bottom of the career ladder long enough to develop institutional knowledge.

---

## Where It Is Happening

| Department | Attrition Rate | vs. Company Average |
|---|---|---|
| Sales | 20.7% | +4.5 points |
| Human Resources | 19.0% | +2.8 points |
| Research & Development | 13.8% | −2.4 points |

Sales is the most critical priority — it is both the highest-risk department and the one where attrition most directly impacts revenue generation.

---

## The Supporting Signal

Employees who left scored consistently lower across all four satisfaction dimensions measured:

| Metric | Stayers (Avg) | Leavers (Avg) |
|---|---|---|
| Job Satisfaction | Higher | Lower |
| Environment Satisfaction | Higher | Lower |
| Work-Life Balance | Higher | Lower |
| Relationship Satisfaction | Higher | Lower |

The consistency across all four metrics suggests systemic dissatisfaction — not an isolated bad experience.

---

## Priority Recommendations

| Priority | Action | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Audit and redistribute overtime in Sales | 0–3 months |
| 2 | Redesign the 0–90 day onboarding experience | 3–6 months |
| 3 | Benchmark entry-level compensation against market | 6–12 months |
| 4 | Implement quarterly satisfaction pulse surveys | Ongoing |

---

## Bottom Line

The data tells a coherent story: this is not random attrition. It is structural, concentrated in identifiable groups and departments, and preventable with targeted interventions. The highest-impact action — addressing overtime distribution — requires no budget increase. It requires a management decision.
