## Finding 1 — The Attrition Headline

**What:**
The organisation lost 238 employees — 16.2% of its 1,470-person workforce — in the observed period.

**How bad:**
Industry benchmarks for voluntary attrition typically sit between 10–15% for stable organisations. At 16.2%, this company is operating above that range, meaning attrition here is a business problem, not background noise. Assuming a conservative replacement cost of 50% of annual salary per employee, and an average monthly income of approximately $6,500:

> **Estimated annual replacement cost: 238 × ($6,500 × 12) × 50% ≈ $9.3M**

**Why:**
No single cause drives the full rate — it is a convergence of workload, compensation, and satisfaction factors that the remaining findings unpack.

**So what:**
This number is the business case for everything that follows. $9.3M in replacement costs is not a rounding error — it is a budget line that HR leadership can take to the executive table. The remaining findings show precisely where to spend to reduce it.

## Finding 2 — Sales and HR Are the Hardest Hit

**What:**
Attrition by department: Sales 20.7%, Human Resources 19.0%, Research & Development 13.8%.

**How bad:**
Sales attrition is 4.5 percentage points above the company average. HR is 2.8 points above. R&D is 2.4 points below. The same organisation has two completely different attrition realities depending on which department an employee sits in.

**Why:**
Sales roles carry the highest overtime exposure and the highest performance pressure in this dataset. HR, despite being the smallest department, shows similarly elevated risk — notable because HR professionals are precisely the people responsible for designing retention strategy. R&D's lower rate likely reflects longer career investment cycles and stronger internal career development pathways.

**So what:**
Sales attrition at 20.7% means the department most responsible for revenue generation is also the least stable. Every Sales Representative who leaves takes client relationships, pipeline knowledge, and onboarding investment with them. A Sales Representative typically takes 3–6 months to reach full productivity after hire. When 1 in 5 Sales employees leaves annually, the department is perpetually operating below productive capacity.

HR at 19.0% deserves separate attention: an HR function experiencing high attrition loses institutional memory of its own policies, processes, and people history — a compounding problem that undermines every other retention initiative.

## Finding 3 — The Overtime Multiplier

**What:**
Employees working overtime leave at 30.6%. Employees not working overtime leave at 10.4%.

**How bad:**
A 3× multiplier is not a marginal difference — it is a structural signal. Of all variables in this dataset, overtime is the single strongest predictor of attrition. The gap between 30.6% and 10.4% is 20.2 percentage points — larger than the difference between the highest and lowest-attrition departments.

**Why:**
Overtime correlates with lower work-life balance scores, higher burnout likelihood, and reduced job satisfaction — all of which appear consistently lower among employees who left. Overtime is not a neutral scheduling decision: it is a retention risk every time it is assigned, particularly when not rotated equitably or compensated adequately.

**So what:**
This is the most actionable finding in this analysis. Unlike age or tenure — which HR cannot control — overtime is a management decision made daily. Recommended intervention:

- Audit overtime distribution by team and manager immediately
- Identify whether overtime is concentrated among specific individuals or spread equitably
- Introduce a threshold trigger: any employee working overtime for more than 8 consecutive weeks automatically enters a manager check-in protocol
- Review overtime compensation structure in Sales specifically

## Finding 4 — The Tenure Cliff

**What:**
Attrition peaks in the 0–1 year tenure band and declines progressively as tenure increases.

**How bad:**
The organisation is investing in hiring and onboarding, then losing employees before recovering that investment. The cost is front-loaded; the benefit — institutional knowledge, client relationships, team cohesion — never fully arrives.

**Why:**
Employees who leave in year one typically cite unmet expectations (the role was not what was described during recruitment), inadequate onboarding support, or early culture shock. These are preventable causes — they reflect a failure of the organisation's early-career experience design, not an inherent characteristic of the employees who leave.

**So what:**
A tenure cliff at 0–1 years is a structural onboarding failure. Recommended intervention:

- Audit current onboarding: do new hires receive a structured first-90-day experience, or are they left to self-navigate?
- Implement 30-60-90 day check-ins for all new hires as a minimum standard
- Assign a named peer mentor for every new employee through their first 6 months
- Examine whether recruitment messaging accurately reflects day-to-day role reality

Fixing year-one attrition has a compounding return: every employee retained past year one is statistically far more likely to remain through year five and beyond.

---

## Finding 5 — The Pay Gradient

**What:**
Attrition decreases as monthly income increases. The lowest income band (Under $3K/month) carries the highest attrition rate; the highest band ($15K+) carries the lowest.

**How bad:**
This is a pipeline problem. Entry-level employees — the organisation's future mid-level talent — are leaving before they fully develop. The organisation is continuously recruiting at the bottom of the career ladder without retaining those recruits long enough to generate a return on that investment.

**Why:**
Low absolute pay, limited perceived growth trajectory, and higher likelihood of overtime concentration at junior levels all converge in the lowest income bands. Entry-level employees have the lowest switching costs (fewer commitments, broader job mobility) and the highest sensitivity to perceived pay inequity.

**So what:**
This finding does not simply mean "pay everyone more." It means the compensation structure at the lowest two income bands warrants a targeted market review:

- Are entry-level salaries competitive with current market rates for equivalent roles?
- Is the promotion timeline from entry-level to the next band clear, achievable, and communicated?
- Are entry-level employees experiencing disproportionate overtime without commensurate pay?

Employees who cannot see a path to higher compensation within a reasonable timeframe will find one at a competitor.

## Finding 6 — The Satisfaction Gap

**What:**
Employees who left (Attrition = Yes) scored lower than those who stayed (Attrition = No) on all four measured satisfaction dimensions: Job Satisfaction, Environment Satisfaction, Work-Life Balance, and Relationship Satisfaction.

**How bad:**
The gap is consistent across all four metrics — not isolated to one dimension. This means the dissatisfaction experienced by employees who left was broad and systemic, not the product of a single bad experience.

**Why (with caveat):**
This is a correlation, not a confirmed causal chain. This dataset captures satisfaction scores at a point in time and does not confirm whether low scores preceded or followed the decision to leave. Both directions are plausible: low satisfaction may drive the departure decision, or the decision to leave may reduce reported satisfaction scores retrospectively. Treat this as a strong signal, not a proven mechanism.

**So what:**
The practical implication is forward-looking rather than retrospective. Quarterly pulse surveys monitoring these four dimensions at the team level could serve as an early-warning system. A team whose average scores are declining across multiple dimensions simultaneously is a retention risk before anyone has submitted a resignation. This converts attrition from a lagging indicator (someone already leaving) into a leading one (someone at risk of leaving).

## Recommendations

### Priority 1 — Immediate (0–3 months)
**Audit and redistribute overtime, beginning with Sales**

- Identify all employees who have worked overtime for 4+ consecutive weeks
- Assess whether overtime is distributed equitably within teams or concentrated in specific individuals
- Introduce a manager check-in protocol triggered by sustained overtime
- Review Sales overtime compensation structure

*Expected impact:* Direct reduction in the 30.6% overtime attrition rate — the fastest-moving and most directly controllable lever available.

---

### Priority 2 — Short-term (3–6 months)
**Redesign the 0–90 day onboarding experience**

- Audit current new-hire experience against best-practice onboarding frameworks
- Introduce structured 30-60-90 day check-in milestones for all new hires
- Assign a named peer mentor for every new employee through month 6
- Review whether recruitment messaging accurately represents role reality

*Expected impact:* Reduction in 0–1 year attrition band — highest ROI retention investment available given the front-loaded cost structure of hiring.

---

### Priority 3 — Medium-term (6–12 months)
**Conduct compensation benchmarking for the Under $3K and $3K–$6K income bands**

- Commission or conduct a market compensation review for entry-level and junior roles
- Define and communicate clear, achievable promotion timelines from entry-level to mid-level
- Assess whether overtime in entry-level roles is being adequately compensated

*Expected impact:* Reduction in lower-income-band attrition over a 12-month horizon as compensation competitiveness improves.

---

### Priority 4 — Ongoing
**Implement quarterly team-level satisfaction pulse surveys**

- Use the four satisfaction dimensions from this analysis as the baseline survey framework: Job, Environment, Work-Life Balance, Relationship
- Report results at team level (not only company-wide) to make them actionable for line managers
- Flag any team whose scores decline across 2+ dimensions in consecutive quarters for manager and HR review

*Expected impact:* Conversion of attrition from a lagging indicator to a leading one. The organisation gains the ability to intervene before departures occur rather than analysing them after the fact.

## Analytical Caveats

1. **Satisfaction causality** — Direction of the satisfaction-attrition relationship cannot be confirmed from this cross-sectional dataset
2. **Missing manager data** — 57 missing values in YearsWithCurrManager limit manager-level analysis
3. **Replacement cost estimate** — The $9.3M figure uses a 50% of annual salary multiplier; actual costs vary by role seniority and market conditions
4. **PerformanceRating** — Restricted to values 3–4 only; insufficient variation for meaningful attrition analysis by performance
5. **Temporal context** — This is a cross-sectional snapshot; longitudinal data would strengthen causal claims
